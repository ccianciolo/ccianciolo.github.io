The Quest

The cloud storage and organization market is young and ripe for development. Even with big name companies filling the space there is room for another player to meet specific needs and enter the fray. Identifying a need in an already competitive market provided a challenge to overcome. The client identified there is potential to do something new in the cloud storage market but did not give much information beyond that. It was up to me, the UX researcher and designer to laser focus my efforts by gathering data and massaging it into a product design to bring to market.

Competitive Analysis

I did a competitive analysis of some of the larger competitors in the cloud storage market to see what was already available. The market is full of different competitors doing very different types of things. Each of the cloud services that were chosen for analysis were identified specifically because they are utilized for different purposes. Each of them saves content to the cloud.

Google Drive stores all sorts of files and is fantastic for collaboration with others. Drive allows for collaboration in real time within content that can be created from within the Google Drive platform.

Pinterest saves links to content and is great for sharing creative “pins”. It is a great place to gain insight into new ideas or gather creative ideas when initially embarking on a project.

Evernote is a note taking application that saves content from the web that is stored within the service. Evernote allows for collaboration as well and has the ability to take photos, record audio and add a sketch along with attaching files to a “note”.

Evernote organizes with a limited notebook system. There are many questions that will need to be asked and through user surveys and analysis of this data a niche market will begin to take shape.

User Research

Initially I decided to work with what I know. In my current job, I work with teachers who have a wide range of needs. I began the process by surveying teachers and tennis players about their cloud storage habits and needs. Teaching, and teaching tennis are two subjects that I know a lot about being a former Director of Tennis and a current Technology Coach working with k-12 teachers. I decided to survey these two areas to work with what I know.

A majority (94%) of the surveyed use Google drive as it is what has been provided to them by their school district or work environment. Knowing that teachers use cloud storage was the foundation, but I needed to drill down deeper to see what needs they may have that could be met by a new solution in the market. The survey data identified some areas that would need to be addressed; organization methods and search ease.

Cloud Storage Usage

At this point I had quality data, but not enough to narrow down the focus of my application design. I designed two user personas based on the demographic data provided through the survey and brainstormed with my team lead. In our conversation we agreed that I seemed more passionate about the Tennis player and teacher demographic but needed data to confirm that instinct. I had an inlking of where this data would go but wanted to back it up with solid info so I developed another 5 question survey. In this second survey I was able to identify that there was in fact a need that was not being met for tennis players and coaches.

Survey Takeaways

84% of the surveyed did not currently have a method of tracking tennis onformation via digital or journaled method on paper.

72% of the surveyed stated an interest in storing tennis related notes and videos to the cloud.

63% of the surveyed were interested in comparing their tennis videos with thos of the pros.

40% of the surveyed were interested in storing articles specific to tennis (already have other methods of doing so).

My instinct seemed to be correct as a need was identified and an interest was shown in a potential note taking app geared specifically towards tennis players. Upon refocusing on a tennis goal and note taking app I did a quick competitive analysis taking a peek at Google Keep to see what the competition was doing in the realm of note taking and where there are areas for entrance into the market I also delved deeper into the user personas to identify the two major users for this app, tennis players and coaches.

User Testing

User tests were then done to see if the user experience was simple as that was the initial goal of designing this application (simple but focused on tennis players and coaches). In the essence of inclusitivity I decided to test on 2 people inside the target audience and one outside the target audience age range. I was able to get testing from a high school aged tennis player and a coach and another tennis player in his 60s.

One super useful comment from the coach related specifically to the organization of the “landing page” inside the app putting most important processes at the top and working down from there. The high school student was able to test his way through the app with no issues. The older tester had some difficulty with the ellipses and I made note of this for later iterations of the app (high fidelity).

Branding

In developing branding for TopSpin cloud I worked with moodboards and looked at classic looks of different companies that have been working with tennis players for years to invoke a familiar feel for tennis players and coaches. I based the brand on the simplicity and clean look of these classics. I worked opaque tennis court or tennis related high quality image into the background of the onboarding screens. This app will be marketed to tennis players and their coaches to allow for tracking goals and improvements while allowing coaches a way to see and give feedback to players. Future iterations will include the ability to mark up videos and create side by side comparisons.

Color and Typography

Each of the colors were selected to invoke the memory and feeling of being on a tennis court and playing tennis. The complementary fonts Bebas Neue and DIN Condensed were chosen because they are clean, blocky fonts that invoke old style tennis signage from the past. The overarching idea of the app is to be simple to use and to bring improvement to players of any level. The icons, colors, and typography work to do just that.

Preference testing

For the main logo I did a preference test to identify if a black outline should be included on the main logo or if white lettering worked better before beginning the process of working on High Fidelity output.Preference testing showed users preferred white text in the main logo. Preference testing did not conclusively show any difference when testing rounded corners for buttons to use within the app so I stuck with my initial choice for corners.

33% of surveyed users chose Black outlined logo

67% of surveyed users chose White outlined logo

33% of surveyed users chose the home screen with a blue court background.

67% of surveyed users chose the home screen with the red clay court background.

HiFi Mockups

High fidelity mockups of each screen of the app were developed with the above research in mind. Did some comparisons of current iOS apps that utilize similar methods to organize files (Spark, Apple Music). Style guides were referenced often to ensure consistency. I utilized what I had developed throughout the wireframe process as a skeleton. Some usability issues were identified here so the entire app was put through a contrast checker which caused some color changes to occur throughout specific screens which were adjusted.

I noted here that naming really matters to organize the files in a meaningful way. Some of my margins were inconsistent. Double checked all margins throughout the design to ensure consistency across all screens. I utilized a numbering scheme to make sure files could be adjusted when feedback was given and exported easily for further use. The images below show progress in design due to feedback from a senior designer on the team.

HiFi User Testing

Taking into account feedback given above about contrast and icons I moved forward with prototyping using Adobe XD. I tested my prototype with a different group of users to see how close my design was to shipping.

User feedback noted a few different adjustments that needed to be made. Some of the hit boxes for touch were not large enough and caused confusion when trying to navigate through the application. The ellipses for note sharing and organization were too small and needed to be adjusted for usability. One of the users noted that the ellipses didn’t make much sense as he hadn’t used similar programs like Google Drive in the past so he wasn’t sure to tap there to gain access to more functions of the app.

I added in a tutorial screen for this which opens only when users first enter the application from the sign up screen. For users who enter from the sign in screen there is a different home screen which does not access the tutorial.

Quest Completed


This research really determined the direction of the app and any processes and design stemmed from it. Details, details, details are what I learned from designing this application.


My initial designs were done as PNG files from Affinity Designer which was great to learn, but if I had it to do over again I would have used XD for the whole project from the start. There is something freeing about just making little tweaks within one piece of software. In my wireframe process I used both Affinity Designer and Adobe XD to develop the prototype.


This was a learning experience because there was a lot of time spent iterating in Affinity and then exporting and pulling into Adobe XD. Also once the files were inputted into Adobe to make changes required going back into Designer OR adding clickable rectangles over the top as “white out” and then adding a new element inside of XD. Some of the more visual elements needed to be designed in Affinity but when designing so many different screens I think Adobe makes things more efficient.


All of the information gathered directed decisions I made throughout the process. The end result was a research based coherent design that can be implemented for design onto the iOS app store.
